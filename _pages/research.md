Our group’s research is broadly focused on optical physics. We study light-generating systems from a physics perspective to uncover new phenomena and functionalities that could enable unique applications. A defining theme of our work is the tight interplay between experiment and predictive modeling of complex light–matter interactions.

Below are representative project areas.

## Programmable control and optimization of the quantum noise of light

![Programmable control of quantum noise](https://sites.coecis.cornell.edu/rivera/files/2026/03/Screenshot-2026-03-05-at-8.26.39-PM.png)

Many intense light sources have noise far above shot-noise levels, which limits sensitivity, imaging contrast, penetration depth, and acquisition speed in applications such as optical coherence tomography. In recent work, we developed a method to control noise in intense beams by exploiting nonlinear-mode correlations and actively tuning relative mode phases at the input (for example with a spatial light modulator).

This optimization framework reduced relative intensity noise in part of a beam by more than 12 dB and can operate in regimes where conventional stabilization approaches struggle near shot noise. A key ingredient is an adjoint model of the multimode generalized nonlinear Schrödinger equation, enabling efficient and accurate prediction of quantum-noise effects in nonlinear waveguides.

- Preprint: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2509.03482
- Code: https://github.com/michaelhorodynski/MultiModeNoise.jl

## Noise-immune quantum correlations of intense light

![Noise-immune quantum correlations](https://sites.coecis.cornell.edu/rivera/files/2026/03/Screenshot-2026-03-05-at-8.25.38-PM.png)

High-intensity lasers typically exhibit strong excess intensity fluctuations. In recent work, we demonstrated generation of intense light with quantum-level noise, even when starting from highly noisy inputs.

The theory developed for this work (quantum sensitivity analysis) showed that the nonlinear dissipation process generating squeezed light is effectively immune to added pump noise over broad conditions. This points to a collective, mode-correlated mechanism where groups of modes can decouple from noise even when individual modes remain noisy.

- Paper: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41566-025-01677-2

## A framework for nanophotonically enhanced scintillators

![Nanophotonic scintillator framework](https://sites.coecis.cornell.edu/rivera/files/2026/03/Screenshot-2026-03-05-at-10.14.46-PM-e1772767091756.png)

We also develop nanophotonic approaches for ionizing-radiation detection. In this direction, we showed that nanoscale patterning of scintillators can strongly reshape and enhance light output.

As one example, photonic-crystal patterning of a scintillator surface produced up to 10× enhancement in emitted light. The design relied on a unified, computationally efficient framework combining first-principles emitter modeling, charged-particle energy-loss physics, and photonic-structure emission analysis (including reciprocity-based methods for tractable simulation).

- Paper: https://www.science.org/doi/full/10.1126/science.abm9293
